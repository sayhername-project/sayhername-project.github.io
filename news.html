How Media Covered #SayHerName

When Black women were killed by police, their stories were often buried or forgotten. In 2015, #SayHerName challenged America to remember. This analysis reveals how news media responded—tracking coverage patterns, key moments, and the outlets that amplified these voices.

In 2015, a movement demanded something different: that we say their names, that we remember, that we refuse to look away. This is the story of how America's news media responded—and who chose to listen.





Our dataset, scraped from MediaCloud, began with 10,759 raw articles. We processed the data through two stages of deduplication. During stage 1, source-level deduplication, we removed duplicate results caused by repeated scrapes or overlapping Google search returns. This returned 7,505 articles. Next, we performed content-level deduplication to remove articles with identical or near-identical content across outlets. This reduced the dataset to 5,823 unique articles.

Part 1

When Did They Start Paying Attention?

Media attention doesn’t appear by accident. It’s shaped by search terms, editorial choices, and whose stories newsrooms consider newsworthy.

While our initial queries related to #SayHerName and Black Lives Matter surfaced almost 50,000 articles from MediaCloud, only about 10,000 appeared when searching for #SayHerName and the names of Black women and girls killed in 2015. That gap is itself part of the story — an absence that reflects how rarely their cases were centered.

The rhythm of news attention reveals patterns of visibility and neglect. In the #SayHerName dataset, the clearest spike occurs in mid-July 2015, when Sandra Bland’s death forced the nation to reckon with yet another tragedy. Outside that moment, coverage remains comparatively sparse. These rises and lulls show not just when people paid attention, but also how seldom they did.

A few dates stand out - days when the loss of a Black woman finally broke through the larger conversation on policing and racial justice. Sandra Bland’s death, in particular, generated a sharp surge in reporting that dwarfs the coverage of other women in the movement.

But the long stretches of quiet are just as revealing. Despite ongoing advocacy, renewed cases, and persistent systemic issues, media attention often receded to near silence. The movement continued; the cameras did not.

Part 2

Who Controlled the Narrative?

In American media, a small number of outlets often determine which stories break through. When a major national paper covers an issue, other outlets tend to follow. But when coverage is driven by smaller digital sites or local reporting, the national press may barely respond.

For #SayHerName, the question becomes even sharper: Who actually told these stories, and what kind of outlets shaped public understanding?

The Power Players


Across 421 news outlets, coverage of #SayHerName was concentrated in just a few hands. The top 10 outlets published 2,121 stories — 28.3% of all articles in the dataset. Expanding to the top 20 brings the total to 3,155 articles, or 42.0% of all coverage.

The remaining 401 outlets accounted for 58.0% of articles, but each with far smaller individual output, making their voices easier to overlook in the national conversation.

Notice who the top contributors actually are: not traditional national newspapers, but digital-native sites, entertainment/celebrity news hybrids, and regional broadcasters. BuzzFeed, Huffington Post, EURweb, local affiliates, and regional news sites dominate the list. These outlets often publish rapidly and in high volume, but they do not carry the same agenda-setting weight as publications like the New York Times, the Wall Street Journal, or major televised networks. This contrast reveals a divergence between proximity and power: local journalists showed up first, but high-volume digital and national media shaped the largest share of the public conversation.

Part 3

Which Voices Reached the Public?

Police violence unfolds at the scale of neighborhoods, cities, and counties — and the first journalists to respond are almost always local reporters. They hear the sirens, show up before official statements, and speak directly with families in the raw aftermath. In most cases, it is local newsrooms that build the first public record. But in the #SayHerName dataset, that early presence did not translate into volume.

National outlets — many of them digital-native rather than legacy institutions — produced roughly half of all articles (50.16%), far outpacing local newsrooms, which accounted for about a quarter (24.99%). A substantial portion of coverage also came from entertainment-adjacent or mixed-format sites classified as “Other” (20.00%), while international and broadcast sources contributed only small fractions.

When Different Media Entered - and Exited


Media attention to #SayHerName varied across outlet types. Local journalists often provided the first coverage, closely connected to the communities affected. For most cases, national and local coverage numbers were roughly comparable, with each type of outlet contributing to the story in different ways.



The Sandra Bland case stands out as an exception. National media quickly amplified the story, producing roughly twice as many articles as local outlets at the peak of attention.

Digital-first and mixed-format sites also played an important role, filling gaps and maintaining visibility when local or national outlets were less active. International and broadcast coverage remained sporadic, appearing primarily for the most widely noticed or globally relevant moments.

Overall, these patterns show how different media types entered and exited stories at different times, shaping which narratives became widely known. Understanding these dynamics helps explain why some cases gained national attention while others remained largely under the radar.

Part 4

How Was the Story Told?

Words matter. The difference between "officer-involved shooting" and "police killing," between "incident" and "violence," shapes how we understand what happened.

Coverage disproportionately highlighted violence with eye-catching words such as "police", "death", "shooting", and "brutality". These emotionally charged terms dominate the headlines, drawing attention to the most dramatic aspects of each case, such as with "6 Things White Feminists Can Do in Response to Sandra Bland and Police Brutality Against Women of Color". Keywords around immediate harm, death, and protest dominate, while words signaling reform, accountability, or societal context appear far less frequently. This imbalance shows that media tended to frame events emotionally, emphasizing drama and immediate consequences rather than structural issues.

Emotional versus action-oriented framing further illustrates these patterns. Headlines using words like “tragedy” or “death” convey emotion, while those demanding “justice” or “accountability” direct readers toward response or action. In the #SayHerName dataset, emotional framing appears most frequently, highlighting human impact, while action framing is less common, signaling fewer calls for systemic change. Together, these patterns show not just what events were covered, but how they were interpreted, shaping public understanding of victims, communities, activists, and institutions.

The keyword data underscores these observations. “Sandra Bland” appears in 23.0% of articles, followed by terms like “police” (14.4%) and “death” (11.5%).

Interestingly, despite the centrality of the #SayHerName movement to these stories, the phrase itself barely appeared in coverage, showing up in only 0.4% of articles. This suggests that while media reported on the cases and highlighted violence, outrage, and tragedy, they rarely connected the reporting to the broader movement explicitly. In other words, the framing focused on individual incidents and dramatic events rather than naming and amplifying the movement itself.

Conclusion

The Bigger Picture

The numbers tell part of the story: a couple thousand articles, a couple hundred news outlets, a largely-single sustained burst of attention.

But numbers alone can't capture what was at stake - the lives of Sandra Bland, Rekia Boyd, Tanisha Anderson, and so many others whose names we were finally beginning to say.

Media coverage matters because it shapes what we know, what we remember, and what we act on.





In 2015, #SayHerName forced media outlets to reckon with a story many had previously ignored. Some rose to the challenge; others published once and moved on.



Local reporters stayed with the stories in their communities. National outlets drove the peaks of attention. Digital publications filled the gaps in between.

The patterns reveal who had power to shape the narrative, whose perspectives were centered, and which stories received sustained attention versus fleeting interest.

Mentions of the movement itself remained limited, reflecting that while the cases were covered, #SayHerName as a unifying frame was rarely invoked. The work of saying their names, of demanding justice, continues.
