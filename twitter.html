Understanding the Voices Behind #SayHerName

Since its emergence in 2015, the hashtag #SayHerName has stood as a powerful call for justice—amplifying the voices and stories of Black women and girls whose encounters with police violence have too often been ignored. This project examines how the movement took shape on Twitter during its formative year, uncovering patterns of participation, emotion, and community that defined its rise.

About This Page

Here, we present the major findings from this analysis:

  • The Rise of #SayHerName — how the hashtag first gained traction during AAPF’s 2015 webinar series Her Dream Deferred.
  • The Evolution of #SayHerName Throughout 2015 — tracing how the movement grew into sustained digital resistance.
  • The Users Behind #SayHerName — exploring the emotional and motivational dimensions of participation.

Together, these insights offer a data-driven portrait of digital activism as interconnected stories, emotions, and calls for justice shaping the online visibility of Black women in 2015.

The Rise of the #SayHerName Movement

In 2015, the African American Policy Forum, cofounded by Kimberlé Crenshaw and Columbia Law School, hosted a weeklong webinar series, “Her Dream Deferred,” dedicated to discussing the state of Black women and girls in the U.S. The first day’s webcast, on March 30, 2015, introduced #SayHerName — concise enough to gain traction across Twitter and beyond.

The Evolution of #SayHerName Throughout 2015

Throughout 2015, #SayHerName became a digital space for memorialization, organizing, and protest. Users tweeted in solidarity, demanding justice for Black women and girls whose stories had long been marginalized, transforming the hashtag into a sustained campaign of remembrance and resistance.

The Users Behind #SayHerName

At the heart of #SayHerName were thousands of users who transformed personal and collective grief into digital action—tweeting 564,925 times in 2015. Data analysis reveals motivational and behavioral patterns—calls to action, expressions of outrage, and messages of mourning—that kept the movement visible and emotionally resonant online.
